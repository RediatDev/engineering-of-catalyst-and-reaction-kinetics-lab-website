Dr. Subhrajit Roy

Currently Dr. Subhrajit is working on the extraction and valorization of lignin from biomass, specifically using pine tree needles. The process begins with air-dried biomass, which is then powdered and subjected to delignification using either a microwave reactor or an oil-bath reactor. Protic Ionic Liquids (PIL) and Deep Eutectic Solvents (DES) serve as solvents for the extraction process. The breakdown of lignocellulosic biomass releases key components like cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, whose polymeric structure is depicted along with major monolignols and bond types. The kinetic model shown describes the reaction dynamics of lignin extraction, providing insights into the efficiency of the process.

Mahendra Meena

Mahendera is currently working on the catalytic conversion of carbon dioxide (CO₂) into valuable chemicals through hydrogenation reactions. It outlines major CO₂ sources, including industrial emissions, fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and the energy sector, which accounts for approximately 25% of global CO₂ emissions. The process utilizes renewable energy-driven hydrogenation to convert CO₂ into olefins, methane (CH₄), and methanol, which serve as essential building blocks for plastics, organic chemicals, food packaging, and other industrial applications. The increasing market demand for light olefins, projected to grow from USD 340 million in 2021 to USD 480 million by 2027, underscores the economic potential of CO₂ valorization in sustainable chemistry.

Rajeev Ranjan

Rajeev is working on the catalytic conversion of carbon dioxide (CO₂) to formic acid (HCOOH) via hydrogenation. The problem statement highlights the thermodynamic feasibility challenges and the need for stable, reusable, and cost-effective catalysts, as current options rely on noble metals. The methodology involves a nickel-based catalyst under high-pressure CO₂ and H₂ conditions in a water solvent at 30–200°C, achieving nearly 100% selectivity for formic acid. Key findings include the successful synthesis of nano-nickel (~21 nm), a high FA formation rate (2245 μmol·g⁻¹·h⁻¹), and stability over multiple recycling experiments. Ongoing research focuses on advanced catalyst synthesis, computational screening, alternative hydrogen sources, and kinetic studies to optimize efficiency and scalability.

Arisha Sharma

Arisha is currently working on Tri-reforming of Methane (TRM), a process that efficiently converts greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄) into synthesis gas (syngas) through a combination of steam reforming (MSR), dry reforming (MDR), and partial oxidation (POM) reactions. TRM offers significant advantages, including effective CO₂ utilization, high CH₄ and CO₂ conversion, and improved catalyst stability due to H₂O and O₂ presence. However, challenges such as catalyst deactivation, endothermic energy requirements, and the need for oxygen separation units must be addressed. Recent advancements focus on MOF-derived catalysts, which offer high surface area, tunable porosity, and enhanced stability for TRM reactions. These catalysts improve syngas yield and catalyst longevity, making TRM a promising route for sustainable energy and chemical production.

Redait Terefe

Rediat is working on production of platform chemicals from biomass is a promising approach for sustainable chemical synthesis, with furfural being a key intermediate derived from biomass through a biphasic system. This process involves the hydrolysis of biomass to xylose, followed by catalytic conversion into furfural, which is then extracted into an organic solvent. Further upgrading of furfural to furfuryl alcohol is achieved via the catalytic transfer hydrogenation (CTH) process, utilizing MOF-derived catalysts. These catalysts, impregnated with nickel (Ni) and subjected to drying, calcination, and reduction, offer enhanced activity, stability, and selectivity for hydrogenation reactions. The integration of MOF-based catalysts in biomass valorization provides an efficient and environmentally friendly route to high-value chemicals with broad industrial applications.

Anjali Garg

Anjali is working on tri-reforming of methane (TRM) is an advanced process that efficiently converts methane (CH₄) and greenhouse gases (CO₂) into syngas (CO + H₂) through a combination of steam reforming (SRM), dry reforming (DRM), and partial oxidation (POM). This process not only enables CO₂ utilization but also achieves high CH₄ and CO₂ conversion, making it an effective approach for mitigating greenhouse gas emissions while producing valuable syngas. To enhance catalyst stability and efficiency, Perovskite derived catalysts have emerged as promising materials due to their high thermal stability and tunablity. These catalysts improve resistance to carbon deposition, addressing key challenges associated with conventional catalysts. The TRM process becomes a more sustainable and economically viable route for syngas production, supporting clean energy and industrial applications.
